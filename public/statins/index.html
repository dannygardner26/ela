Panel A

Background

Research Question

How do statins affect LDL cholesterol levels and cardiovascular event risk in patients aged 40–75 years old?

Why This Matters

Heart disease is the leading cause of death for both men and women, causing 1 in every 3 deaths in the United States. An estimated 920,000 lives were lost to cardiovascular disease in 2023, with $417.9 billion in associated healthcare costs (Centers for Disease Control and Prevention [CDC], 2024).

What Is LDL Cholesterol?

LDL (low-density lipoprotein) is a particle in the blood made up of lipids and proteins that carries fats through the bloodstream. Elevated LDL leads to plaque buildup in arteries (atherosclerosis), increasing risk of coronary artery disease, stroke, and heart attack (Cleveland Clinic, 2024).

The Disease Pathway

Step 1

Excessive LDL particles circulate in the blood

Step 2

LDL cholesterol is oxidized, acting abnormally in the body

Step 3

Oxidized LDL disrupts endothelium function, forms deposits, triggers inflammation

Step 4

Vessel tears or becomes blocked — heart attack, stroke, or blood clot

What Are Statins?

Statins are the most prescribed medication in order to combat high cholesterol, with around 200 million people worldwide taking them. Atorvastatin is the most prescribed drug in all of America. They have been FDA-approved since 1987 and work by lowering LDL cholesterol and stabilizing arterial plaque (Johns Hopkins Medicine, n.d.).

Mechanism of Action

1 — Inhibit Enzyme

Statins inhibit HMG-CoA reductase, the rate-limiting enzyme in hepatic cholesterol production

2 — Reduced Synthesis

Liver produces less cholesterol, causing movement of sterol regulatory element binding proteins (SREBPs)

3 — More LDL Receptors

SREBPs increase transcription of the LDL receptor gene, producing more receptors

4 — LDL Clearance

More LDL receptors cause greater uptake and clearance of LDL from the bloodstream (Feingold, 2024)

Research Findings

  • Log-linear relationship between statin dose and LDL-C reduction: doubling dose reduces LDL-C by 5.6–6.2% (Oni-Orisan et al., 2018)
  • Benefits consistent regardless of age, sex, or baseline LDL-C (CTT Collaborators, 2012)
  • No increase in cancer incidence or non-vascular mortality (CTT Collaboration, 2010)

Benefits

  • 22% reduction in CV events
  • 10% reduction in mortality
  • Cost-effective medication
  • Consistent effects over time

Risks & Side Effects

  • Muscle pain (~1%)
  • New-onset diabetes (10–36%)
  • Rhabdomyolysis (rare)
Conclusion

For most patients with elevated cardiovascular risk, the benefits of statins clearly overshadow the potential risks. Statins are highly effective at lowering LDL cholesterol (30–50%), with strong evidence supporting a 22% reduction in major cardiovascular events per 38.67 mg/dL decrease in LDL-C.
