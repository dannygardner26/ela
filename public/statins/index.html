What Is LDL Cholesterol?

LDL (low-density lipoprotein) carries fats through the bloodstream. Elevated LDL leads to plaque buildup in arteries (atherosclerosis), increasing risk of coronary artery disease, stroke, and heart attack.

What Are Statins?

Statins are the most prescribed medication to combat high cholesterol, with around 200 million people worldwide taking them. Atorvastatin is the most prescribed drug in all of America. FDA-approved since 1987, they work by lowering LDL cholesterol and stabilizing arterial plaque.

Mechanism of Action

01
Inhibit Enzyme

Block HMG-CoA reductase, the rate-limiting enzyme in cholesterol production

02
Reduced Synthesis

Liver produces less cholesterol, activating SREBPs

03
More Receptors

SREBPs increase LDL receptor gene transcription

04
LDL Clearance

More receptors = greater uptake of LDL from blood

(Feingold, 2024)

Research Findings

  • Log-linear relationship between dose and LDL-C reduction: doubling dose reduces LDL-C by 5.6–6.2%
  • Benefits consistent regardless of age, sex, or baseline LDL-C
  • No increase in cancer incidence or non-vascular mortality

Benefits

  • 22% reduction in CV events
  • 10% reduction in mortality
  • Cost-effective treatment
  • Consistent effects over time

Risks

  • Muscle pain (~1%)
  • New-onset diabetes (10–36%)
  • Rhabdomyolysis (rare)
Conclusion

For most patients with elevated cardiovascular risk, the benefits of statins clearly outweigh the potential risks. Statins are highly effective at lowering LDL cholesterol (30–50%), with strong evidence supporting a 22% reduction in major cardiovascular events per 38.67 mg/dL decrease in LDL-C.
